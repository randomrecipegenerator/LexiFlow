The Narrative

What happened to Elena Rodriguez, and how the system failed her.

18:30 — Arrival

Elena Rodriguez arrives at Mount Sinai ER with 103.2°F fever, HR 115, qSOFA score 2/3 — indicating 10% in-hospital mortality risk. Surviving Sepsis Campaign guidelines require 3-Hour Bundle initiation.

19:15 — The Order

ER resident orders blood cultures and IV Vancomycin. The order is entered. The standard-of-care clock starts ticking.

20:00 → 09:30 — 14-Hour Gap

Zero antibiotics administered. No vital sign checks. No follow-up assessments. The MAR shows no documentation for 13.5 hours. The patient who met sepsis criteria becomes invisible.

10:00 — The Crash

Code Blue called. BP 70/40. Septic shock. Prolonged hypotension and high-dose vasopressors lead to irreversible tissue death in both feet.

May 15 — Outcome: Double below-knee amputation. A catastrophic outcome from a preventable delay.

🤖 AI-Generated Cross-Examination Questions

  1. "Doctor, you testified that you 'personally confirmed' the start of antibiotics at 19:45. Can you explain why the hospital's own electronic MAR, which timestamped in real-time, shows no such administration occurred for the next 14 hours?"
  2. "You characterize a qSOFA score of 2 — which carries a 10% risk of in-hospital mortality — as 'stable'? Is that the standard definition of stability used at Mount Sinai?"
  3. "Does the hospital's Sepsis Protocol require the '3-Hour Bundle' implementation for patients meeting 2/3 qSOFA criteria?"
  4. "If the '3-Hour Bundle' was not completed within 14 hours, would you agree that is a deviation from the hospital's internal safety guidelines?"
  5. "Who was the nurse assigned to Bed 4 between the hours of 20:00 and 08:00?"
