## Why Medical Chronologies Matter More Than Ever

A well-constructed medical chronology is the backbone of any personal injury or medical malpractice case. It tells the story of the plaintiff's injury and treatment in a clear, chronological sequence — giving judges, juries, mediators, and opposing counsel an immediate understanding of what happened, when it happened, and what the medical record says about it.

But creating a medical chronology is painstaking work. A single PI case can involve 2,000–5,000 pages of medical records across multiple providers. Organizing those records into a coherent, citation-backed timeline requires hours of focused effort — and even then, human reviewers miss between 8% and 12% of critical events.

This guide walks you through the step-by-step process of creating a medical chronology that holds up under scrutiny, whether you're building one manually or leveraging AI-powered medical chronology software.

## What Is a Medical Chronology?

A medical chronology is a document that organizes a plaintiff's medical treatment into a sequential timeline. Each entry documents a specific clinical event — a visit, a procedure, a diagnostic test, a medication change — along with:

- **Date of service**
- **Provider name and facility**
- **Diagnosis or presenting problem**
- **Treatment or procedure performed**
- **Medication prescribed or administered**
- **Key clinical findings** (lab results, imaging findings, vital signs)
- **Source citation** (document name, page number, exhibit number)

The chronology serves as a roadmap through the medical record, allowing attorneys, experts, and mediators to quickly locate relevant information without reading thousands of pages.

## Step 1: Gather and Organize Medical Records

Before you can build a chronology, you need complete records. This means issuing medical record authorizations and subpoenas to every provider who treated the plaintiff — including:

- Primary care physicians
- Emergency departments
- Specialists (orthopedics, neurology, pain management, etc.)
- Surgical centers and hospitals
- Imaging centers
- Physical therapy and rehabilitation providers
- Pharmacy records

**Pro tip:** Create a provider tracking sheet that lists every facility, the date records were requested, the date received, and whether they're complete. Missing records are the single most common reason chronologies need to be rebuilt.

## Step 2: Create Your Chronology Framework

Before diving into the records, set up your chronology structure. The standard format includes these columns:

| Date | Provider | Event Type | Description | Key Findings | Citation |
|-----|----------|-----------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| 2026-01-15 | Mercy Hospital ED | Emergency Visit | Patient presented with lower back pain after MVA | X-ray: L1 compression fracture | Records_p015 |
| 2026-01-16 | Dr. Alan Roth, Ortho | Initial Consultation | Diagnosis: L1 compression fracture, recommended conservative care | MRI confirms acute fracture | Records_p032 |

**Consistency is critical.** Every entry should follow the same format. Use standardized event types (e.g., "Emergency Visit," "Surgery," "Imaging," "Office Visit," "PT Session") so the chronology can be sorted and filtered.

## Step 3: Read and Extract in Chronological Order

This is the most time-consuming step. Working through records chronologically — rather than by provider — ensures you capture the full clinical picture.

For each document:

1. **Identify the date of service** — Confirm it's the actual treatment date, not the date the note was written
2. **Extract the chief complaint** — What brought the patient in?
3. **Document the objective findings** — Lab values, imaging results, physical exam findings
4. **Note the assessment and plan** — What did the provider diagnose and recommend?
5. **Record medications and dosages** — Including changes from previous visits
6. **Capture referrals** — Who was the patient referred to, and why?
7. **Cite the source** — Document name or number and specific page

**Manual accuracy benchmarks:** Experienced paralegals achieve approximately 88–92% accuracy in event extraction. The most commonly missed items are medication changes, subtle abnormal lab values, and referrals to specialists.

## Step 4: Cross-Reference for Completeness

After extracting all events, cross-reference your chronology against the full record set. Common gaps include:

- **Missing follow-up visits** — A patient was told to return in 2 weeks but there's no corresponding visit
- **Discrepant dates** — The ED note says "discharged 1/16" but the hospital census shows discharge on 1/18
- **Unreported imaging** — A referral for an MRI exists but the report isn't in the records
- **Medication gaps** — A prescription was written but there's no evidence it was filled or taken

Flag these inconsistencies. They may indicate incomplete records — or they may be case-critical timeline issues.

## Step 5: Produce the Final Chronology

The final chronology should include:

1. **A cover page** with case name, plaintiff name, date range, and total page count
2. **The chronology table** with consistent formatting throughout
3. **A summary section** highlighting key dates and events
4. **An index of providers** with contact information
5. **An exhibit list** cross-referencing all source documents

**Format consideration:** Most firms use Excel or Word for manual chronologies, but dedicated medical chronology software provides better consistency, searchability, and exhibit linking.

## Step 6: Review and Verify

Before the chronology enters the case file, it should be reviewed by a second person — preferably an attorney or senior paralegal who wasn't involved in the initial extraction. The reviewer should:

- Spot-check 20% of entries against source documents
- Verify all critical dates (injury date, surgery dates, discharge dates)
- Confirm that the summary accurately reflects the chronology
- Check for internal inconsistencies

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

| Pitfall | Impact | Prevention |
|---------|--------|------------|
| Inconsistent date formatting | Confusion, credibility issues | Use ISO format (YYYY-MM-DD) throughout |
| Missing citations | Cannot verify entries at trial | Cite every entry to specific page |
| Provider name variations | Duplicate entries | Standardize provider names upfront |
| Subjective language | Defense attacks credibility | Use objective clinical language only |
| Incomplete records | Chronology must be rebuilt | Track provider responses diligently |

## FAQ

### What is the best format for a medical chronology?

The standard format is a table with columns for date, provider, event type, description, key findings, and source citation. Most firms use Excel or Word, but dedicated medical chronology software provides superior formatting, searchability, and linking.

### How long does it take to create a medical chronology manually?

Experienced paralegals typically spend 4–8 hours per 1,000 pages of medical records. A typical PI case with 3,000 pages requires 12–24 hours.

### What's the difference between a medical chronology and a medical summary?

A medical chronology is a sequential timeline of every clinical event with source citations. A medical summary is a narrative overview that synthesizes key findings and conclusions. The chronology is the raw data; the summary is the analysis.
