AI Ethics in Legal Intake: A Practical Framework for New Jersey Trial Lawyers

📅 July 10, 2026⏱ 8 min read🏷 AI ethics New JerseyFrom $29/mo

New Jersey trial lawyers face a distinctive medical malpractice environment: a 2-year statute of limitations, a strict Affidavit of Merit requirement (N.J.S.A. 2A:53A-27), modified comparative negligence (50% bar), and a $500K cap on punitive damages. AI-powered intake must be guided by the New Jersey Rules of Professional Conduct (NJ RPC).

1. Client Confidentiality (NJ RPC 1.6)

NJ RPC 1.6 requires lawyers to maintain confidentiality of client information. AI platforms must encrypt all data at rest and in transit (AES-256 + TLS 1.3), prohibit model training on client data or medical records, provide signed BAAs for PHI handling, and maintain complete audit trails for all data processing.

2. Competence (NJ RPC 1.1)

Comment [8] to NJ RPC 1.1 requires attorneys to understand the benefits and risks of relevant technology. New Jersey firms must establish protocols to verify AI-generated findings, understand system limitations, train personnel, and document all AI-assisted screening decisions. LexiFlow's source-cited chronology and merit reports facilitate this oversight.

3. The Affidavit of Merit Advantage

N.J.S.A. 2A:53A-27 requires plaintiffs to file an Affidavit of Merit from a similarly-credentialed expert within 120 days of the defendant's answer — a procedural hurdle that can be challenging without rapid case evaluation. AI-powered medical chronology and merit analysis can accelerate the evidentiary foundation for the required expert opinion by identifying standard-of-care deviations, documenting the injury-causation chain, and providing citation-backed findings within minutes.

4. Punitive Caps & Comparative Fault

New Jersey caps punitive damages at $500K or 5x compensatory damages (whichever is greater) under the Punitive Damages Act. The modified comparative negligence rule bars recovery at 50% plaintiff fault. AI intake can screen for liability risks and punitive exposure during initial qualification, helping firms make informed case acceptance decisions.

5. Supervision (NJ RPC 5.3)

New Jersey firms must establish written AI governance policies, train non-lawyer personnel, implement quality controls, and ensure attorney oversight of all AI-assisted work product. The supervising attorney retains full responsibility under the NJ RPC.
