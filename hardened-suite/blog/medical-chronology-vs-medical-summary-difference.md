## Two Documents, One Goal — But Different Purposes

Every personal injury case generates a mountain of medical records. To make those records usable in litigation, attorneys need two distinct documents: a medical chronology and a medical summary.

While both serve to organize and present medical information, they serve fundamentally different purposes — and using the wrong one at the wrong time can cost you credibility with a judge, jury, or mediator.

This guide explains the difference between a medical chronology and a medical summary, when to use each, and how to build both efficiently.

## What Is a Medical Chronology?

A medical chronology is a **sequential timeline** of every clinical event in the plaintiff's treatment history. Each entry records a specific event — an office visit, a procedure, a diagnostic test — with objective clinical data and a citation to the source document.

**Key characteristics:**
- Organized chronologically by date of service
- Documents every clinical encounter, not just significant events
- Uses objective clinical language — no interpretation or opinion
- Includes complete source citations for every entry
- Typically formatted as a table or spreadsheet

**Best used for:**
- Trial preparation and witness examination
- Expert witness collaboration
- Mediation and settlement conferences
- Deposition cross-reference
- Insurance defense discovery responses

## What Is a Medical Summary?

A medical summary is a **narrative synthesis** of the plaintiff's medical history. Rather than listing every event, it summarizes key findings, identifies trends, and presents the clinical story in a readable format.

**Key characteristics:**
- Written in narrative prose rather than table format
- Synthesizes and interprets — it identifies what matters
- Organized by body system, injury type, or treatment phase
- Includes conclusions and opinions about the medical evidence
- Shorter — typically 3–10 pages versus 20–100+ for a chronology

**Best used for:**
- Demand letters to insurance carriers
- Settlement brochures and mediation briefs
- Plaintiff's expert report preparation
- Case evaluation and intake screening
- Communication with clients about their medical history

## The Differences at a Glance

| Factor | Medical Chronology | Medical Summary |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Format** | Table / spreadsheet | Narrative prose |
| **Scope** | Every clinical event | Key findings and trends |
| **Tone** | Objective — cites facts only | Analytical — includes interpretation |
| **Length** | 20–100+ pages | 3–10 pages |
| **Citations** | Every entry cited | Key points cited |
| **Time to create** | 4–8 hrs per 1,000 pages | 1–3 hrs total |
| **Primary audience** | Attorneys, experts, mediators | Insurance adjusters, clients, mediators |
| **Best use case** | Trial prep, discovery | Demand letters, settlement |

## When to Use a Medical Chronology

**Trial preparation** is where the chronology earns its keep. When you're standing in front of a jury examining a medical witness, you need instant access to every relevant encounter — in chronological order, with page-specific citations. A chronology gives you that.

**Expert witness collaboration** also demands a chronology. Your medical experts need to review the complete record in sequence to form reliable opinions. A summary, by condensing the record, may inadvertently omit facts that change the expert's analysis.

**Deposition cross-examination** is another chronology stronghold. When a defense medical examiner's testimony contradicts the record, a well-built chronology lets you impeach with precision — "Doctor, you testified there was no mention of back pain at the January 15 visit, but the record at page 15 says…"

## When to Use a Medical Summary

**Demand letters** are the primary use case for medical summaries. Insurance adjusters and defense counsel don't need to see every office visit. They need to understand the injury, the treatment, the prognosis, and the damages — presented efficiently and persuasively.

**Settlement brochures and mediation briefs** likewise benefit from the summary format. The goal here is persuasion, not exhaustive documentation. Tell the medical story. Highlight the key findings. Make the case for why the damages justify the demand.

**Client communication** is an often-overlooked use case. Your client doesn't need to read a 100-page chronology of their own treatment. A 2-page medical summary written in plain language helps them understand their own case — and makes them a better witness at deposition.

## Can You Have Both?

The best-prepared plaintiff firms maintain both documents for every active case.

A typical workflow:

1. **Build the chronology first.** Extract every clinical event from the records. This ensures nothing is missed.
2. **Use the chronology to verify the record is complete.** Cross-reference against your provider list. Flag any gaps.
3. **Draft the summary from the chronology.** Now that you have the complete timeline, synthesize the key findings into a narrative.
4. **Cross-link them.** Every statement in the summary should reference the chronology page where supporting detail can be found.

A modern approach is AI medical chronology software with built-in summary generation. The AI extracts the complete timeline automatically, then generates a narrative summary based on your selected focus areas — liability, damages, causation, or all three.

## The Cost Difference

Because a chronology requires documenting every single clinical encounter, it takes significantly more time to produce than a summary.

| Document Type | Manual Time | AI-Assisted Time |
|--------------|-------------|------------------|
| Chronology (3,000 pages) | 12–24 hours | 2–3 hours |
| Summary (from chronology) | 2–3 hours | 15–30 minutes |
| Summary (from scratch) | 3–5 hours | 1–2 hours |

The key insight: building the chronology is the heavy lift. Once you have a complete, accurate chronology, producing the summary is relatively quick — especially with AI tools that convert timeline data into narrative form.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Submitting a chronology when a summary is expected.** Many insurance adjusters and mediators find chronologies overwhelming. Ask what format the recipient prefers.

**Mistake 2: Using a summary as a substitute for a chronology.** At trial, you'll need the granular detail that only a chronology provides. Don't skip it.

**Mistake 3: Mixing interpretation into a chronology.** A chronology should report what the record says, not what you think it means. Save analysis for the summary.

**Mistake 4: Failing to cross-reference.** The strongest practice is to build both documents and link them — summary claims should be traceable back to specific chronology entries.

## FAQ

### Which should I create first — chronology or summary?

Always build the chronology first. The timeline of every clinical event is the foundation. The summary is a synthesis of that foundation.

### Can one document serve both purposes?

Not effectively. A chronology is too dense for demand letters. A summary lacks the granularity needed for trial. Most plaintiff firms maintain both.

### Do I need a medical expert to create a chronology?

No. A well-trained paralegal can build a complete medical chronology. The analysis and interpretation in a medical summary may benefit from medical expert input, but the chronology itself is a factual document.
