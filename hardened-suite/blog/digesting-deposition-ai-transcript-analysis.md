## The 4-Hour Deposition Digest Problem

Every trial lawyer knows the ritual. A deposition ends at 4 PM. By 9 AM the next morning, someone needs to have it "digested" — summarized, organized by issue, cross-referenced against the medical records, and flagged for inconsistencies.

For most firms, this means a senior associate or paralegal spends 4–6 hours per deposition hour doing exactly that. For a 6-hour deposition, that's 24–36 hours of post-deposition work.

Now multiply by the 20–40 depositions a mid-sized plaintiff firm takes per month.

The math is brutal: 480–1,440 hours of billable time per month spent not on strategy, but on digestion.

## What "Digesting a Deposition" Actually Means

In litigation practice, digesting a deposition involves several discrete tasks:

1. **Full transcript review** — reading every page and marking key testimony
2. **Issue organization** — grouping testimony by legal topic (liability, damages, causation, etc.)
3. **Contradiction detection** — comparing testimony against prior statements, records, and other depositions
4. **Medical record cross-reference** — verifying testimony against treatment notes, imaging reports, and expert opinions
5. **Witness credibility assessment** — noting evasions, inconsistencies, and demeanor indicators
6. **Summary production** — creating issue-specific digests for use at trial or mediation
7. **Exhibit correlation** — linking testimony to specific exhibits in the record

Each of these tasks is painstaking manual work. And each is susceptible to human error — missed contradictions, overlooked exhibits, and inconsistent issue coding across a multi-deposition case.
